Reviewer 3 of ICRA 2026 submission 4499

Comments to the author
======================

The authors proposed a work that addresses the problem of
clearing a path for a large vehicle in a cluttered
environment filled with movable obstacles. With the
proposed work, the authors tackle two challenges that
advances the prior work: Manipulation planning problem
considering the physical dimensions of a robot and
Collaborative pushing problem. Both challenges are known to
be complex, thus solving a problem that is a combination of
the two is an interesting research.

The proposed method, PushAround, is designed to tackle
coupled problems of high-level obstacle clearing and
pushing dynamics in the low-level through the following
approaches. The method first constructs a graph based on
the geometry of the given obstacles, termed as WCCG. The
authors propose that because a WCCG is made under the
constrains of the geometry of the large vehicle, a search
over the graph guarantees feasible dynamics.

Through experiments in both simulation and real hardware,
the authors exhibited the computational performance and
scalability to a large number of objects (30).

While the framework is interesting, the manuscript seems to
be missing, if not misleading, essential descriptions of
the method.

Since the goal of the method is to plan collaborative
obstacle clearing by multiple (small) robots, collaborative
pushing is an essential part of the work. Section II.C is
the corresponding description of the problem, but the
subsection seems rather incomplete. 

- How the admissible set of modes are found from contact
geometry and friction limits?

- Assuming each robot takes exactly one contact point, how
does the method assign each contact point to each robot?

- During transition or robots, how is a collision-free path
found? (with the fact the space is not grid, which
algorithm?)

- How does a swap of contact points guarantee a resolution
to a set of colliding paths? Would it be still valid for
more than two robots?

- The subsection describes a pushing mode, but I failed to
understand what &#8706;&#937;_m^N refers to. (Specifically,
what does &#8706; mean here?) 

- What is wrench profile? By 'body-frame force', does it
mean the net force from some of the contact points? If so,
how is the pushing force (magnitute, assuming normal to the
surface) at each contact point found to calculate the
intended body-frame force? 

- The first sentence seems to be missing another
sentence/paragraph before it since it is a conclusion that
'the robots may actively reconfigure' the movable objects. 

In Remark 4, what is 'ModeTable' and how is it defined
(missing definition)?

Below are parts of the work I wish the authors to clarify:
- For geometric planning of the large vehicle, does it
always plan to go through the midpoint of each
bridge-bridge edge (gap)? If so, would a larger gap be
making the resulting path longer?

- In the 'Generalization', the authors claim that it can be
generalized to diverse robots. Are they limited to
rectangular robots? Can they be bigger than a given movable
object? Are they limited to holonomic robots? Are they
limited to have at most one contact point?

- In the Section III.C, how are the 'batch of pushing
strategies' found before they can be validated using
simulations?

- In showing scalability, it would be clearer how scalable
the method is if the results include the scalability trend
relative to a metric (i.e. as number of object or the size
of the space increases).

Minor comments,

- In equation (4) and (5), it seems like m_k is supposed to
be a subscript of a missing &#937;.

- Across the manuscript, the symbol &#960; is termed with
multiple names. In the problem statement, it is termed as
'overall schedule', but in Section III.C, &#960; is named
as a 'partial pushing strategy'.

- Space omit in C. "contact modes,and forces"

- In Fig 5, more detailed description would help readers to
understand better.

- To help readers to understand better, it would be better
to state definitions of each term when they first appear
(i.e. push strategy)

- In Section III.C, are 'push strategy' and 'pushing
strategy' the same/interchangeable term?
