¥subsection{Pushing Mode Generation and Evaluation}¥label{subsec:push}
For a selected frontier gap‾$g$, this subsection constructs a set of candidate
pushing strategies
$¥Xi_g ¥triangleq ¥{(¥Omega_m,¥mathbf{v},¥boldsymbol{¥xi})¥}$,
where‾$¥mathbf{v} ¥triangleq (v_x,v_y,¥omega)¥in¥mathbb{R}^3$ is a
short-horizon pushing direction for the movable obstacle‾$¥Omega_m$ induced by‾$g$, and
$¥boldsymbol{¥xi}¥triangleq(¥mathcal{C}_m,¥mathbf{u})$ is the associated pushing
mode in‾¥eqref{eq:transition}.



%==============================================
¥subsubsection{Direct and Recursive Pushing Tasks}
For ¥emph{direct push}, let‾$¥Omega_m$ denote one of the two movable obstacles adjacent to‾$g$. For
each such obstacle, a small set of candidate body velocities
$¥{¥mathbf{v}_m^k¥}_{k=1}^{K_m}$ is sampled with a bias toward enlarging the
gap‾$g$, while a few angular components are included to account for local
geometry.
However, if the swept region of a direct push intersects
movable blockers, ¥emph{recursive push} tasks are further required.
Namely, for
each out-layer blocker‾$¥Omega_m$, the nominal direction moves it away
from both the swept region
and the reference gap sequence‾$¥Pi^¥star(g)$,
with the candidate velocities‾$¥{¥mathbf{v}_m^k¥}_{k=1}^{K_m}$.
The recursive pushes aim to
move the blocker out of the swept region rather than directly open the gap.
Recursion therefore creates a bounded dependency chain which prioritizes the
final blocker‾$¥Omega_m$, as shown in
Fig.‾¥ref{fig:direct_recursive_push}.

¥subsubsection{Pushing Mode Generation}
At a search state‾$¥mathbf{s}$, a pushing mode is generated for each candidate
direction‾$¥mathbf{v}_m^k$ and obstacle‾$¥Omega_m$. In this subsubsection,
contact tuples, object velocities, forces, and wrenches are expressed in the
body frame of‾$¥Omega_m$. For a contact tuple‾$¥mathcal{C}$, its
multi-directional force feasibility is
¥begin{equation}¥label{eq:mode-feasibility}
¥mathrm{J}_{¥texttt{MF}}^m(¥mathcal{C},¥mathbf{v}_m^k)
¥triangleq
¥sum_{¥mathbf{v}_d¥in¥mathcal{D}_m^k} w_d
¥min_{¥mathbf{u}¥in¥mathcal{U}_m(¥mathcal{C})}
¥|
¥mathbf{q}_m(¥mathcal{C},¥mathbf{u})
+¥mathbf{q}_m^¥mu(¥mathbf{v}_d)
¥| ,
¥end{equation}
where‾$¥|¥cdot¥|$ is a weighted $¥ell_1$ wrench norm,
$¥mathcal{D}_m^k$ is a finite perturbation set around‾$¥mathbf{v}_m^k$,
and‾$¥mathbf{q}_m^¥mu(¥mathbf{v}_d)$ is the quasi-static friction wrench of $¥Omega_m$; 
the inner minimization is a small Linear Program (LP) 
over the admissible contact forces‾$¥mathcal{U}_m(¥mathcal{C})$.
A state- and direction-dependent contact set
$¥mathcal{A}_m^k(¥mathbf{s})¥subset¥partial¥Omega_m$ is obtained from the
robot-reachable boundary of‾$¥Omega_m$ by velocity--force compatibility
filtering, as illustrated in Fig.‾¥ref{fig:mode_generation}. Starting from
cached layouts, if available, and random initializations in
$¥mathcal{A}_m^k(¥mathbf{s})$, the contact tuple is refined by sequential greedy
replacement. At the $¥ell$-th greedy step, let‾$¥mathcal{C}^{¥ell}$ be the
current tuple, and let‾$¥mathcal{C}_{r,c}^{¥ell}$ be obtained by replacing its
$r$-th contact with‾$c$. The best replacement is
¥begin{equation}
c^¥star
¥in
¥arg¥min_{c¥in¥mathcal{A}_m^k(¥mathbf{s})}
¥mathrm{J}_{¥texttt{MF}}^m
¥bigl(¥mathcal{C}_{r,c}^{¥ell},¥mathbf{v}_m^k¥bigr).
¥label{eq:greedy_mode_update}
¥end{equation}
The greedy refinement sweeps over contact slots until
$¥mathrm{J}_{¥texttt{MF}}^m$ no longer decreases. 
Among initializations, the
best local tuple is selected as‾$¥mathcal{C}_m^{k,¥star}$, and
$¥mathbf{u}_m^k$ is obtained by the inner minimization in
¥eqref{eq:mode-feasibility} for‾$¥mathbf{v}_m^k$. 
This yields $¥boldsymbol{¥xi}_m^k=(¥mathcal{C}_m^{k,¥star},¥mathbf{u}_m^k)$. 
The mode is inserted into a persistent ¥textsc{mode-cache}, 
indexed by discretized shape, mass, available robot force, and pushing direction, 
and used only as a warm start for later similar queries.
The best few pairs‾$(¥mathbf{v}_m^k,¥boldsymbol{¥xi}_m^k)$ with
$¥boldsymbol{¥xi}_m^k¥in¥Xi_m(¥mathbf{s})$ are retained for gap‾$g$ and passed
to simulation evaluation.

¥subsubsection{Pushing Strategy Evaluation}
For a current state‾$¥mathbf{s}$ and a candidate strategy
$(¥Omega_m,¥mathbf{v},¥boldsymbol{¥xi})¥in¥Xi_g$, a short-horizon rollout evaluates the
transition in‾¥eqref{eq:transition} under the selected mode.
As shown in Fig.‾¥ref{fig:mode_generation}, a strategy is
accepted only if the robots reach the pre-contact poses,
maintain stable contact, and induce obstacle motion above threshold. The
simulation returns:
¥begin{equation}
  (¥mathbf{s}',¥,¥delta_{¥texttt{T}},¥,¥delta_{¥texttt{J}})
  ¥triangleq
  ¥mathsf{EvalSim}¥big(¥mathbf{s},¥,(¥Omega_m,¥mathbf{v},¥boldsymbol{¥xi})¥big),
  ¥label{eq:sim}
¥end{equation}
where‾$¥mathbf{s}'$ is the successor state given the strategy;
$¥delta_{¥texttt{T}}>0$ is the
time cost; and‾$¥delta_{¥texttt{J}}$ is the control effort.
For efficiency, the pushing rollout may be bypassed when the mode has been validated before 
and the swept region of‾$¥Omega_m$ is collision-free with other obstacles.
