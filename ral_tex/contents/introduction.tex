\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}
In many real-world settings, the passage of large vehicles or transport units is
obstructed by movable obstacles such as pallets, boxes, or equipment, motivating
mobile robot teams to clear traversable corridors and enable safe passage. This
capability is critical in cluttered and unstructured environments such as
warehouses, disaster sites, and dense urban spaces. Conventional path planning
methods assume static obstacles and fail once direct routes are
blocked~\cite{liu2023path}. Navigation Among Movable Obstacles~(NAMO) has been
studied as a means of creating new routes by pushing, pulling, or rotating
obstacles~\cite{stilman2005navigation}, yet most methods abstract away physical
feasibility. Factors such as robot dimensions, obstacle size and mass, contact
geometry, applied forces, and coupled pushing dynamics are typically ignored,
producing plans that are \emph{geometrically valid but physically unrealizable}.
As shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:snapshots}, a single large robot often cannot reach
obstacle boundaries, whereas a small robot lacks pushing power. Cooperative
pushing by several small robots provides a viable alternative, but introduces
strong physical coupling: simultaneous pushes can trigger chained motions of
multiple obstacles, making prediction and planning substantially more complex.

\subsection{Related Work}\label{subsec:intro-related}

NAMO has been studied as an extension of motion planning in cluttered
environments. Classical methods planned explicit obstacle-displacement
sequences through pushing, pulling, or
rotating~\cite{stilman2005navigation,stilman2007manipulation}, while later work
introduced heuristic search~\cite{stilman2007manipulation} and graph- or
sampling-based formulations for larger workspaces~\cite{yao2024local}. Recent
methods extend NAMO to multi-agent settings where teams of robots clear
passages collaboratively~\cite{tang2024collaborative,ren2025search}. However,
most NAMO planners still idealize robots as point agents with unlimited
actuation and neglect robot size, obstacle mass, and realistic dynamics, leading
to plans that are physically unrealizable.

Collaborative pushing provides a physical mechanism for such scenarios and has
been widely investigated in multi-robot manipulation. Foundational studies
examined pushing mechanics and the limit surface
model~\cite{goyal1989limit,lynch1992manipulation}; more recent work addressed
force synchronization~\cite{ni2023progressive}, contact stability and slip
margins~\cite{liu2025physics,chen2015occlusion}, and cooperative transport of
large payloads in structured environments~\cite{ni2024physics,wang2006multi}.
Learning-based approaches further explore emergent coordination in cluttered
settings~\cite{feng2025learning}. These studies demonstrate effective
multi-robot cooperation but are generally limited to single-object tasks and
assume strict collision avoidance with surrounding obstacles. Inter-object
interactions and coupled dynamics among many movable obstacles therefore remain
largely unaddressed.

Physics-informed planning has emerged to improve physical feasibility. Several
methods use physics engines to validate candidate manipulations~\cite{lin2019efficient}
or embed contact simulation directly in the planning loop~\cite{rouxel2024multi}.
Other approaches incorporate dynamic models into non-prehensile manipulation
planning~\cite{xue2023guided,saxena2023planning} or apply learning with physics
priors and differentiable physics for contact-rich
tasks~\cite{ni2023progressive,liu2025physics}. These methods emphasize physical
reasoning, but often decouple high-level planning from low-level feasibility
checks or remain confined to single-object manipulation. Consequently, they do
not scale to collaborative path clearing in cluttered environments with many
movable obstacles.

\subsection{Our Method}\label{subsec:intro-our}
As shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:overall}, this work introduces \textbf{PushAround}, a
physics-informed framework for collaborative multi-robot pushing that constructs
traversable corridors for large vehicles in clutter. The central component is a
hybrid search that couples the high-level choice of frontier gaps with low-level
push tasks specifying the moved object, contact mode, and short-horizon velocity.
The framework integrates three modules: a W--clearance connectivity graph~(WCCG)
for passage certification, a frontier-gap ranking module that predicts the
remaining cost to connect the start and goal, and a recursive push sampler that
turns each selected gap into candidate push tasks. The sampler first proposes
pushes for movable objects adjacent to the selected gap; when the swept motion is
blocked, it recursively samples pushes for the blocking objects and passes the
resulting local clearing tasks to contact-mode generation and simulation rollout.
This focuses simulation on actions that improve WCCG connectivity and accepts
only rollouts satisfying robot, obstacle, and contact constraints.

The contributions are twofold: {(I)} a unified framework that couples
W--clearance reasoning, collaborative pushing, and physics-informed verification
for executable path clearing in cluttered environments; and {(II)} systematic
validation showing improved feasibility, efficiency, and density scalability over
existing NAMO and collaborative clearing methods.
