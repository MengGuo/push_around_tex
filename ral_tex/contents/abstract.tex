\begin{abstract}
The passage of large vehicles in cluttered environments is often blocked by
movable obstacles, motivating mobile robot teams to proactively clear
traversable corridors. Existing Navigation Among Movable Obstacles (NAMO)
approaches mainly plan abstract obstacle-displacement sequences, but often
neglect physical feasibility, including robot dimensions, obstacle mass,
contact geometry, and required pushing forces. This abstraction can produce
plans that are geometrically valid but physically unrealizable. This work
introduces PushAround, a physics-informed framework that couples geometric
corridor reasoning with simulation-validated collaborative pushing. The method
builds a W--clearance connectivity graph (WCCG) for fast passage tests,
extracts and ranks reachable frontier gaps, and expands a search tree whose
node value combines the predicted cost-to-go with the progress induced by the
latest validated push. Candidate push tasks are generated from local clearance
requirements, contact-mode priors, velocity deduplication, and short-horizon
parallel simulation. Thus, computation is focused on pushes that are likely to
improve WCCG connectivity, while each accepted action remains executable under
robot, object, and contact constraints. Simulations, density-scaling tests, and
hardware experiments demonstrate robust corridor construction in dense clutter
and physically consistent multi-robot execution.
\end{abstract}
