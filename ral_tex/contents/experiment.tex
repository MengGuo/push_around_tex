\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}
Numerical experiments evaluate PIHS and its implementation as the ClutterBreaker
policy. The algorithms are implemented in \texttt{Python3} and
\texttt{PyBullet}~\cite{coumans2019} on a computer with an Intel Core
i9-14900KF CPU and an NVIDIA RTX 4080 GPU. Simulation and hardware videos are
provided in the supplementary material.

\subsection{Numerical Simulations}
\label{subsec:sim}

\subsubsection{Setup and Configuration}\label{subsec:sim-setup}
As shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:simloop}, the simulation uses a step of
$\Delta t=1/240$\,s and a control period of $1/40$\,s. Robots are modeled as disk
or box pushers with dimensions consistent with the W--clearance requirement.
Movable obstacle masses are uniformly sampled from $[5,15]$\,kg, while
immovables have zero mass. A trial is successful when a W--clear path connects
start and goal after execution. The nominal workspace is an $8{\times}5$\,m scene
with two fixed bars and a mixed pool of curved and polygonal movables, including
rings, ellipses, X/T/L/diamond shapes, arrows, rectangles, and cylinders. Each
expansion runs WCCG presearch, samples from the first gap of the best predicted
sequence, and keeps at most $16$ validated children. The queue follows
\eqref{eq:node_value}, and task generation uses velocity deduplication,
geometric quick-pass filtering, ModeTable reuse, and parallel short-horizon
simulation.

\subsubsection{Baselines and Ablations}\label{subsec:comparisons}
Four external planners are evaluated under the same W--clearance and contact
models. \textbf{DFS-WCCG} is a simulation-in-the-loop depth-first search guided
by WCCG but restricted to four fixed axis-aligned pushes per movable obstacle.
\textbf{SL-Push (offline)} clears blockers by minimal displacements along a
straight or waypointed route without physics. \textbf{SL-Push (sim)} validates
those route-level pushes with physics rollouts. \textbf{Rec-NAMO} combines
Dijkstra routing, local push decomposition, and edge pruning. Internal ablations
remove major modules of PushAround: \textbf{w/o presearch} disables frontier gap
ranking; \textbf{w/o ModeTable} discards structured contact-mode priors;
\textbf{w/o quick-pass} forces full-horizon rollouts; and \textbf{w/o reinsertion}
prevents revisiting promising but temporarily failed nodes.

Table~\ref{tab:main_ablation} shows that PushAround achieves the highest success
rate, $97.5\%$, with the lowest combined planning and execution time. DFS-WCCG
reaches only $25.0\%$ success because its fixed push set causes exponential
branching. SL-Push offline is fast but often physically invalid, while SL-Push
sim improves feasibility at heavy rollout cost. Rec-NAMO is faster than
simulation-heavy baselines but frequently fails to establish a complete
W--clear corridor. The ablations show that presearch and ModeTable reduce
unnecessary simulations, while quick-pass and reinsertion improve runtime and
robustness.

\subsubsection{Density Scalability}\label{subsec:density}
A separate density-scaling suite evaluates the planner in an $8{\times}8$\,m
workspace with $N\in\{15,20,25,30,35,40,45\}$ movable obstacles and five random
seeds per density. For a fixed seed, denser instances are generated by adding
objects to the lower-density layout, which isolates density from scene identity.
For planning-time curves, every unsuccessful trial, including plan failure and
timeout-pruned rows, is assigned the $400$\,s cap before averaging.

As shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:density_scaling}, PushAround keeps $100\%$ success
across all tested densities and increases from $2.6$\,s at $N=15$ to
$49.2$\,s at $N=45$ in capped mean planning time. In contrast, Rec-NAMO drops to
$0\%$ success from $N=35$, while SL-Push and DFS-WCCG remain at only $20\%$
success at $N=45$ under the same cap convention. The ablation without recursive
local clearance stays competitive at moderate densities but falls to $40\%$
success at $N=45$, showing that recursive blocker clearance is mainly needed in
heavily packed scenes.

\subsection{Hardware Experiments}\label{subsec:hardware}

\subsubsection{System Description and Results}\label{subsec:exp-description}
Experiments are conducted in a $5\,\mathrm{m}\times6\,\mathrm{m}$ motion-capture
workspace with two UGVs and six movable cardboard obstacles, as shown in
Fig.~\ref{fig:hardware}. Motion-capture poses are streamed to \texttt{PyBullet}
for online execution and visualization. In a representative run, WCCG presearch
completes within $10$\,s and clears five gaps through $64$ node expansions with
maximum search depth~$3$. The robots rotate a T-shaped object, translate adjacent
rectangles, and push a cylinder to establish a final W--clear corridor.
Occasional Mecanum-wheel drift near $45^\circ$ causes small deviations, but
online replanning restores WCCG progress. The sequence confirms that the
optimized contact modes and local pushes are physically executable on hardware.
